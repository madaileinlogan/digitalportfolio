Internship Overview

Students will participate in a required 72-hour internship experience before senior year. An internship provides students the opportunity to work closely with a business, non-profit or government agency on a meaningful project. The goal of the internship is to build upon professional skills, discover opportunities for the future and provide students with an exploratory and valuable real-world experience.

Abstract

The purpose of this career exploration report was to identify two possible futures careers, research them, and find four feasible internships in the area. The careers chosen to investigate and find internships for were an intelligence analyst and neurologist. To learn more about each occupation, essential questions were written; these questions helped to guide the research and ensure that the report included all the relevant and important information. Then, the critical subjects of salary, daily responsibilities, necessary qualities, work environment, education, and possible college majors were explored for each profession. The most prevalently used websites were O*NET, Northeastern University, International Relations EDU, Highland Hospital, and Prospects. A combination of government, informational, and university sites were used. Finally, internships were investigated, focusing on the Dayton area and the sought-after work environment. The most promising internship is the Air Force Cognitive Neuroscience Research Lab because it combines research, developing medical interventions, and break-through neurology. Next steps include looking for colleges that are top in the relevant majors for the career and contacting the potential internships.

Focus of Internship

The focus of the internship is to introduce Bayesian statistics. The exploration will be facilitated by studying the Monty Hall game show and developing an Excel simulator for contestant choices and outcomes.


Student Responsibilities

The student will be self-directed and develop a basic understanding of conditional probability. The understanding of conditional probability will be used to develop the Bayes theorem and then apply the learnings to the Monty Hall problem.


Expectation of the Student

The student will develop a PowerPoint presentation explaining conditional probability and Bayes theorem. The presentation will then show how the Bayes theorem can be applied to the Monty Hall problem. An Excel spreadsheet model will be developed to demonstrate empirically that contestants are twice as likely to win the contest if they change their door selection.

Reflection

During my internship, I was tasked with demonstrating how Bayes' Theorem could be applied to the Monty Hall Problem to understand data analytics better. Before learning about Bayes' Theorem, I first researched conditional probability. I found that conditional probability is the probability that an event will occur, given that another event has already transpired. For instance, to represent the probability of event A occurring given that event B has already happened, it would be written as P(A|B). This equation is articulated as "the probability of event A given event B." If solving for the probability of event B given that event A has occurred, it would be written as P(B|A). According to conditional probability, P(A|B) = P(A∩B)/P(B), meaning that the probability of event A given B is the probability of both event A and B happening divided by the probability of event B, and vice versa for P(B|A). When this equation is rearranged, it can also be defined as P(A|B) = (P(B|A) * P(A))/P(B), also known as Bayes' Theorem. Bayes' Theorem can be more helpful than the conditional probability to find the likelihood of a first event, given that the second has already occurred.
In the Monty Hall Game Show, a contestant is presented with three doors. Behind one door, there is nothing, behind another is a goat, and behind the final door is the prize. The contestant chooses between Door #1, #2, or #3. The contestant is then shown the contents behind a door they did not select, one that does not contain the prize. I created an Excel simulator with the goal of finding a pattern that could increase the chance of a contestant winning the prize by selecting the correct door. In my simulator, I randomly generated the door number that the contestant selects and the door number that the prize is behind. If the door that the contestant chooses and the door that the prize is behind are the same numbers, then the contestant will win without changing the door. If this does not happen, Monty Hall opens the door for the contestant that does not contain the prize. I counted the number of times that the door was changed, and the contestant won and solved for the probability of this happening. I did the same for the chance of the contestant not changing the door and winning.
When the contestant is introduced to the three doors, there is a 1/3 chance that the prize is behind any of the doors. The contestant then selects a door with this 1/3 probability of the prize being behind it, and Monty Hall opens one of the other doors. The door does not contain the prize, meaning there is now a 2/3 chance that the prize is behind the final door. I can conclude from this that ideally 1/3 of the time, the prize will be behind the door if the contestant does not change, and 2/3 of the time, the prize will be behind the final door, meaning that the contestant must change. As the sample size increased in my Excel simulator, the probability more accurately represented 1/3 for keeping the door and 2/3 for changing the door.
One of the most challenging parts of this internship was completing it remotely. I often had questions or didn't understand a problem and did not have someone I could ask right away. For instance, in Entry 1, I was trying to answer "How many receipts showed a purchase of both cookies and milk (event B and A)?". I had assumed that there were receipts with neither milk nor cookies for these problems. This meant that I had a variable in every answer and ended with an expression rather than a percentage. Unbeknownst to me, this particular question was unsolvable if neither was an option. After spending a long time considering how to simplify the equation into an expression without enough information, I finally emailed my mentor, asking for some clarification. The following morning, I decided to do some research and noticed that none of the examples of conditional probability I looked at considered a situation where neither event happened. I went back to the questions and tried again, finally finding numbers that made sense. My mentor emailed me later on, confirming my conclusion. Despite struggling with not having answers instantly, I learned patience and how to research and discover things for myself, helping me to fully comprehend concepts on my own.
This internship introduced me to statistics and data analytics. I have found that I enjoy the math behind data analytics and how it can be used to draw conclusions from large amounts of data. I plan to take data science courses in college to learn more about collecting and applying information and explore how this applies to computer science.
